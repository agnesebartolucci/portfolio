Making the Invisible Visible

→ visit the website

Online censorship consists of controlling or removing specific content, accessible to the public, by governments or platforms that want to regulate them. The policies of the latter are becoming increasingly numerous and detailed as the production of visual content grows exponentially. As this phenomenon involves more users, it becomes one of the most discussed topics: Why are some contents censored? Who censors them? Where is the boundary between appropriate and inappropriate content? Sometimes censorship can act silently, leaving users unaware, while other times it can be blatantly evident, prompting them to seek ways to respond to or bypass it.

Our research investigated how censorship is applied to images differently based on the content and the platform's typology through digital methods. The result is displayed in “Making the Invisible Visible,” a website where users can interact with eight different visualizations aimed at providing a comprehensive view of this phenomenon.

2020 — University project

Team: Antonella Autuori, Elena Catani, Mattia Cittadino, Anna Gazza, Barbara Vanoli

Faculty: Ángeles Briones, Gabriele Colombo, Michele Mauri, Simone Vantini, Salvatore Zingale

Skills: Data Visualization Design, Information Design, Qualitative Research, Digital Methods, Data Analysis, UX/UI Design

My contribution: once we established the areas of interest and the identity guidelines together, I primarily focused on research questions 03 (fine arts) and 05 (erotic magazines). I managed the entire process, beginning with defining the research questions and collecting data, and concluding with designing the visualization and identifying the main findings.

"Making the Invisible Visible" is a collection of eight interactive visualizations on the topic of censorship. The research is organized into three levels of depth. The first delves into the types of censored content and the various visual languages employed for censorship. The second examines the responses elicited by censorship, including anti-censorship movements and methods users employ to circumvent it. Lastly, the third level explores the most covert aspect of the phenomenon: the individuals behind it.

↑ Research Question 01 - Mapping the most frequently mentioned places around the world censored on Google Maps.

↑ Research Question 03 - Visualizing the most censored artists and artworks on social media.

↑ Research Question 05 - Analyzing self-censorship techniques employed by erotic magazines to navigate Instagram policies.

↑ Research Question 07 - Visualizing the typical profile of a human moderator using global job listings on LinkedIn.
