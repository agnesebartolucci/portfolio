Red Blood Tomatoes

An infopoetry is a form of data-art, based on visual metaphors that allow designers to show more than the data, tell a story, and provide an overview of a phenomenon. This one aims to tell the living and health conditions of the braccianti who are underpaid workers enrolled by agricultural industries. Their job is seasonal, and it depends on regional harvesting. They work approximately 8 to 12 hours per day, and their salary is often less than 30€ a day. They are often mistreated or abused by the so-called caporali (people who illegally recruit them), and some of them die of fatigue or violence: in the last six years, around 1500 deaths have been recorded. Some of these workers come to Italy just for a harvesting season. Others are locals in poverty, and many are migrants without a residency permit who live in slums near the fields. However, one thing connects them all: the conditions they live every day to pick up fruit and vegetable that end up on our shelves at the lowest price possible.

"Red Blood Tomatoes" consists of 200 photos of various chopped tomatoes cans; each can represent 200 braccianti. The first blow means that there are 40,000 workers at risk of exploitation, the second blow that of these, 10,000 are forced to live in inhuman conditions, and the third blow that the last 5,000 are already in slave-like states.

↑ The can label written a provocative message among the other information like ingredients and energetic values.

This infopoetry uses a metaphor with an ambivalent meaning. On the one hand, it deals with one of the most iconic products of Italian culture (chopped tomatoes), linking it to a bloody image. On the other hand, concerning pop art highlights the consumerist side of the market, through one of the foods best known for the resulting exploitation, again the tomato.

↑ The artwork has been exhibited during the DensityDesign Open Day on February 13th, 2020, and remained on public display for the following month.

The primary data and information sources for this project were the articles “Are your tinned tomatoes picked by slave labour?” and “Stop the exploitation of migrant agricultural workers in Italy”, together with the country study related to Italy in the latest Global Slavery Index.
