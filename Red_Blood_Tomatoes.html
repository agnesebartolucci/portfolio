Red Blood Tomatoes

Infopoetry is a form of data art, based on visual metaphors that allow designers to show more than the data, tell a story, and provide an overview of a phenomenon.

"Red Blood Tomatoes," crafted for DensityDesign Open Day, sheds light on the harsh realities endured by underpaid agricultural workers, known as braccianti. Their seasonal employment, reliant on regional harvests, entails long workdays of 8 to 12 hours, earning meager wages, often less than 30€ per day. They are vulnerable to mistreatment and abuse by illegal recruiters (caporali), with approximately 1500 deaths recorded in the last six years due to fatigue or violence. These workers, including migrants and impoverished locals, endure inhumane conditions to supply inexpensive produce.

In this infopoetry, I used photography as a medium, depicting the act of hammering a can repeatedly.

2020 — University project

Faculty: Ángeles Briones, Gabriele Colombo, Michele Mauri, Simone Vantini, Salvatore Zingale

Skills: Data Visualization Design, Information Design, Graphic Design, Data Analysis, Exhibition Design

"Red Blood Tomatoes" comprises 200 photos of various chopped tomato cans, each representing 200 braccianti. The first blow signifies that 40,000 workers are at risk of exploitation, the second that 10,000 are subjected to inhumane conditions, and the third that 5,000 are already in slave-like states.

↑ The can label I designed contains all the classic information, but embedded within are various provocative messages.

This infopoetry uses a metaphor with an ambivalent meaning. On the one hand, it deals with one of the most iconic products of Italian culture (chopped tomatoes), linking it to a bloody image. On the other hand, concerning pop art highlights the consumerist side of the market, through one of the foods best known for the resulting exploitation, again the tomato.

↑ The infopoetry was exhibited during the DensityDesign Open Day on February 13th, 2020, and remained on public display for the following month.

The primary data and information sources for this project were the articles “Are your tinned tomatoes picked by slave labour?” and “Stop the exploitation of migrant agricultural workers in Italy”, along with the country study related to Italy in the latest Global Slavery Index.
